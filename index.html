Weekly Rhythm
The operating cadence from June 9 through August 29. Every week follows the same structure — once the habit is built, the engine runs itself.

The Weekly North Star

Every week has one question that drives all decisions: "Which programs have open spots and what are we doing about it in the next 72 hours?" The Monday check-in answers that question. Everything else in the week flows from the answer.

The Engine Day — Most Important
  • 8am: Send weekly Spot Check email 20 min
  • Check enrollment numbers from all LDs 10 min
  • Post scarcity Story for any program under 80% 5 min
  • Check Google Ads + Meta ad performance 10 min
  • Follow up with any open inquiries 10 min
  • Post on NextDoor for any open spots 5 min
Tuesday – Thursday
Content + Community
  • Receive LD clips → post best one as Story by 4pm Daily
  • Monitor Facebook groups, answer camp questions 10 min
  • If any program still under 70% Wed: send targeted urgency blast to past attendees of that program 30 min
  • Capture content at camp if on-site Ongoing

Kid of the Week + Next Week Prep
  • Noon: Post Kid of the Week on Instagram 15 min
  • Draft next Monday's Spot Check email 20 min
  • Submit email draft to Tommy for approval 5 min

Weekend
Minimal — Async Only
  • Tommy reviews + approves Monday email draft Sat

Monday: ~60 min. Tuesday–Thursday: ~25 min/day. Friday: ~60 min. Weekend: minimal async. Total: ~3.5–4 hours per week to run the entire marketing engine during camp season. This is executable alongside everything else Macy is doing on the ground.

Camp Week Playbook
12 weeks of camp. Each week has a clear marketing action. The goal for every single week is 100% fill rate by the Monday it starts.

The Gap Fill Rule

If any program is below 80% capacity with 2 weeks to go — a targeted urgency email goes to past attendees of that program. If below 70% with 1 week to go — a scarcity Story goes up the same day AND Macy posts on NextDoor and the Facebook groups. No spot sits unaddressed for more than 72 hours.

Monday March 23 · Solo Presentation
Macy's Pitch Flow
Walk the big bosses through this plan in 20–25 minutes. Lead with the problem, build to the strategy, close with execution confidence. Every section has a talking point.

Macy's Positioning Walking In

You're not presenting ideas — you're presenting a system. The difference is confidence. Every section has a "why," a "how," and an owner. When Tommy and Libby ask "who handles that?" the answer is always clear. That's what makes this pitch land.

1
Open with the goal — one sentence
"Our goal this summer is simple: sell out every program, every week, across all three locations. This plan shows exactly how we get there." Don't over-explain. Let the confidence land before the detail.
~1 minute
2
The situation — where we are right now
Registration is open. Early bird closed. We have a warm audience in our email list and Instagram, a strong foundation of alumni families, and community relationships most businesses would pay for. We're not starting from zero — we're activating what we already have.
~2 minutes
3
Part 1 — Segmented email (lead with the age unlock example)
Start with the example Tommy will immediately connect with: "A family whose child just turned 7 has been doing Beach camp for 3 years. They don't know their kid is now eligible for Surf, Ocean, Volleyball, and Skate. We send them one email this week — and 25–40% of those families register for a new program." Then walk through the 11 segments and the Spot Check cadence.
~6 minutes
4
Part 2 — Schools (lead with "we don't need cold calls")
"We already donate to MBEF, RBEF, HBEF, and eight other school foundations. That relationship gets us into 15+ schools without a single cold outreach to a principal. We design the flyer in Canva, print 1,000 at Costco for $60, deliver in person in May." Simple, credible, actionable.
~3 minutes
5
Part 3 — Communities (lead with the reach number)
"South Bay parent Facebook groups have a combined reach of 15,000–30,000 local parents. We can't advertise in them — but our staff and happy parents can post as themselves. A NextDoor Business profile is free and takes 15 minutes to set up. Facebook Marketplace Services listing is free and almost no camps are using it."
~3 minutes
6
Part 4 — Paid Ads (lead with the budget)
"Two ads. $250–350 per month total. Google Search captures parents already searching for camps — highest purchase intent possible. Instagram Feed reaches new South Bay families who don't follow us yet. If Google Search generates one additional registration per week, it pays for itself entirely."
~3 minutes
7
Close with the execution system — show the weekly rhythm
"Here's what this looks like week to week. Monday I send the Spot Check email and check enrollment. Tuesday through Thursday I post daily Stories from the LD content feed. Friday I post Kid of the Week and draft next week's email for Tommy's approval. That's 3.5 hours a week to run the whole engine." This is where they go from impressed to confident.
~4 minutes
8
Close with two asks — budget approval + approval process
End with two specific asks: (1) "I need approval to spend $350–450/month on Google Ads, Meta Ads, and print." (2) "I'll submit email drafts and ad creative to Tommy by Friday each week — can we agree on 24-hour turnaround for approvals so Monday sends stay on schedule?" Specific asks get specific answers.
~2 minutes

If They Ask "What's the ROI?"

Answer with the referral math: "200 registered families, 10% referral rate, 20 new registrations at $350 average = $7,000 from one email and a box of tees. Google Ads at $200/month, one registration per week = $350 per week revenue against $50 weekly ad cost. The organic channels — email, NextDoor, schools — are effectively free."

If They Ask "What If We Don't Have Time?"

Answer with the rhythm: "The system is designed for 3.5 hours per week during camp season. The heaviest lift is April setup — after that it's a weekly cadence. The Location Directors handle daily content. Lisa handles replies. I handle everything else. It's already accounted for."
